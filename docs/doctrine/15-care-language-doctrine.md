# Doctrine 15 — HOTMESS Care Language

**Locked 2026-05-29 — Phil Gizzie.**
Status: applies retroactively to every surface that already ships and prospectively to everything that will.

---

## 0. The rule

> **Care shouldn't sound sanitised. It should sound like nightlife people taking care of each other in nightlife language.**

That's the whole doctrine. The sections below are tools for enforcing it — tests, vocabulary, examples, forbidden moves. The rule itself is the spine.

## 1. Scope — what this doctrine governs

D15 is not a routing tone guide. It is the emotional grammar of HOTMESS as a whole. It governs:

- **Care** — Care Suite, aftercare beacons, comedown states, post-night UX
- **Ghosted** — grid copy, profile descriptions, boo language, mutual-state messaging
- **Onboarding** — splash, age gate, vibe selection, profile setup, visibility prompts
- **Routing** — D14 mode subtitles, route copy, arrival prompts, navigation cues
- **Check-ins** — safety timers, "got home?" prompts, contact-notify language
- **SOS** — trigger copy, status messages, contact-side notification language
- **Quiet-mode UX** — incognito, low-signal states, lay-low surfaces
- **Marketplace** — listings, HNH MESS product descriptions, checkout copy
- **Push notifications** — every notification body and title, every channel
- **Live presence** — Go Live confirmations, peer-discovery overlay, cold-start states
- **Moderation** — removal notices, warnings, appeals language
- **Radio interstitials** — between-track copy, DJ-voice transitions, station ID

If a surface delivers care, observation, or relational meaning, D15 governs it. If a surface is pure clinical instruction (PEP dosage, emergency numbers, regulatory disclosure), D15 yields to clinical precision — see §6.

## 2. The smoking area test

The single most important diagnostic:

> Would this line sound right in the smoking area outside a venue at 4am?

If yes — it ships. If no — it gets rewritten.

The test runs on every copy string before it lands. Designer wrote it, marketer wrote it, developer wrote it, AI wrote it — doesn't matter. Smoking area test or rewrite.

The test is honest about what HOTMESS users are doing when they read this copy. They are not in a wellness session. They are on a night, between nights, at the edge of a night, or coming down from one. The voice of the app meets them there.

## 3. Additional tone tests

The smoking area test is the master. These are supplementary checks:

- **The mate test.** Would a close mate actually say this? In their own voice, not in a paid-actor voice.
- **The 4am test.** Does this make sense at 4am? Does it acknowledge that 4am is what's happening?
- **The hangover test.** Would the reader want to read this hung over? If it would feel patronising tomorrow, it's wrong tonight.
- **The shame test.** Does this make the reader feel observed or judged? Care should never feel surveilled.
- **The eject test.** Does this make the user feel ejected from the night? (D14 §0 — never eject.)

## 4. The approved lexicon

Phil's locked vocabulary. These are the words HOTMESS care uses.

**Nightlife states.** The states users actually inhabit, named directly.
- afters · comedown · reset · regroup · slowdown · lay low · cool-off · take five · low signal

**Spatial.** The geometry of the city as nightlife knows it.
- corridor · pocket · zone · anchor · stop · landing · warm light

**Care registers.** Care that sounds like care, not therapy.
- safe stop · hold point · still open · somewhere softer · step outside

**Conversational.** The shortest, most powerful lines. These are the ones nightlife people actually say.
- you good? · need a minute? · stay close · no pressure

The best HOTMESS copy is almost always shorter than the alternative. "You good?" beats "How are you feeling tonight?" every time.

## 5. Forbidden vocabulary

Words and phrases that fail by default. The list isn't exhaustive — it's a starting set. The rule (§0) is the source of truth; the list is enforcement.

**Universally forbidden (don't ship, ever):**

- **wellness** — anywhere, in any context
- **self-care** — as a noun phrase in copy (clinically OK in code comments and admin)
- **journey** — when used to describe a user's state ("your recovery journey," "your wellness journey")
- **safe space** — over-used, sanitised, NGO-coded
- **wellbeing** — as a marketing or care register word
- **mindfulness** — anywhere
- **support nearby** / **support available**
- **reach out** — as a CTA
- **centre yourself** / **ground yourself** — wellness-coded
- **room** — unless it is a literal physical room (a hotel room, a venue room). Never "chat room," "sober room," "safe room."

**Forbidden in care contexts (institutional voice):**

- "Your wellbeing matters to us."
- "We're here for you."
- "Support is available."
- "Resources are available."
- "Please reach out if you need help."
- "Sorry to interrupt, but..."
- "We hope you're doing OK."
- "Recovery support nearby."
- "Sober space available."

**Soft-coded phrases that look fine but fail:**

- **soft landing** — wellness-coded despite sounding right; "landing" alone is fine
- **sober space** — NGO copy
- **recovery anchor** — wellness-coded
- **quiet space** — sanitised unless it's literally describing a physically quiet room

When in doubt, run the smoking area test. If it still passes after honest reading, ship.

## 6. Where clinical precision overrides

D15 governs care voice. It does not override clinical accuracy where accuracy genuinely matters.

The line is:

- **Facts and clinical instruction:** clinical voice. PEP within 72 hours. Dosage. Emergency numbers. Regulatory disclosures. These keep their precision.
- **Everything around the facts:** HOTMESS voice. The frame, the affordance, the surrounding copy, the navigation, the confirmation, the silence.

Example: a chemsex aftercare beacon's body might read

> 56 Dean Street. Walk-in for HIV exposure within 72h.
> Open until 7. Step inside.

The "walk-in for HIV exposure within 72h" is clinical precision — never softened, never made vague, never replaced with "support is available." The "step inside" is HOTMESS register — direct, present, lived-in.

That's the seam. Honour both sides.

## 7. Examples — wrong vs right

The forbidden vocabulary list explains the rule. These examples show how it actually plays out across surfaces.

**Routing / care on route (D14 §4–5):**

- ❌ "Recovery support available nearby."
- ✅ "Still open if you need it."

- ❌ "Sober space within walking distance."
- ✅ "Warm light, two blocks."

- ❌ "Safer route option available."
- ✅ "Low signal route home."

**Onboarding:**

- ❌ "Welcome to our supportive community."
- ✅ "You're in."

- ❌ "Set your wellbeing preferences."
- ✅ "What do you need?"

- ❌ "We're here for your recovery journey."
- ✅ "No pressure."

- ❌ "Tell us about yourself to get the most out of HOTMESS."
- ✅ "Who are you tonight?"

**Check-ins:**

- ❌ "Safety check-in completed successfully."
- ✅ "You're home. Good."

- ❌ "Your trusted contacts have been notified of your status."
- ✅ "They know."

- ❌ "Did you arrive home safely?"
- ✅ "You good?"

**SOS:**

- ❌ "Emergency support has been activated."
- ✅ "Help's on the way."

- ❌ "Your designated emergency contacts are receiving an alert."
- ✅ "They're calling now."

**Comedown / aftercare:**

- ❌ "Take a moment to centre yourself."
- ✅ "Take five."

- ❌ "Find peace in this difficult moment."
- ✅ "Lay low. We've got you."

- ❌ "Self-care resources are available in your area."
- ✅ "Somewhere softer nearby."

**Live presence / Go Live (D12 cold-start):**

- ❌ "Be the first to join our community tonight."
- ✅ "You're the first live in [district] tonight."

- ❌ "No nearby members are currently active."
- ✅ "Quiet right now. Stay close."

**Moderation:**

- ❌ "This content may have violated our community guidelines."
- ✅ "This crossed a line."

- ❌ "Action has been taken in accordance with our policies."
- ✅ "We removed it."

- ❌ "We appreciate your cooperation in maintaining our community standards."
- ✅ (Send nothing. Silence is fine here.)

**Push notifications:**

- ❌ "Your wellbeing matters — check in with HOTMESS."
- ✅ "You good?"

- ❌ "Don't forget your self-care today."
- ✅ (Don't send this notification at all. D15 §9.)

## 8. Care without sanitisation — the spine

The doctrine's core observation:

**Care does not get louder by being softened.** It gets louder by being terse, specific, and grounded in the actual situation.

- "You're 3am tired" is more caring than "You may be experiencing fatigue."
- "Lay low" is not unkind — it is accurate and respectful.
- "Still open" is more caring than "support resources are available 24/7."
- "Step outside" is more caring than "would you like to take a moment for yourself?"

The roughest registers can be the most caring. The polished registers can be the most distant. HOTMESS picks the rough register because the user is in the night, not in a waiting room.

A care line that **acknowledges the night honestly** ("4am, no judgement") is more caring than a line that **pretends the night isn't happening** ("we're here whenever you need us").

## 9. Never sound institutional

A separate clause because this is the failure mode HOTMESS care most often drifts into.

D15 forbids any copy that sounds like:

- NHS leaflet phrasing ("If you or someone you know is struggling...")
- Grant report language ("Our service delivers...")
- HR-speak ("We value your contribution...")
- Wellness SaaS marketing ("Your daily wellbeing check-in is ready")
- Recovery program steps ("Step 1 of 12...")
- Therapy app prompts ("How are you feeling today on a scale of...")
- Pharmacy product copy ("This product is intended to support...")

If a copy line would not be out of place on any of those surfaces, it's wrong for HOTMESS — even if the literal information is accurate.

The test: read it aloud. If it sounds like something an institution would write, rewrite it until it sounds like something a person would say.

## 10. Surface-specific rules

Same doctrine, different registers per surface. The rule (§0) is constant.

**Ghosted.** Terse. Sexy. Direct. No therapy language. Mutual-boo is the social contract; copy reinforces it without explaining it.

**Care suite + aftercare beacons.** Nightlife register. Short. "Still open." "Stay close." "Warm light, two blocks."

**Onboarding.** Minimal lift. Don't sell wellness. Sell presence. The first thing a user reads should not feel like a wellness app onboarding screen.

**SOS.** Clinical precision where it matters (999 numbers, PEP windows). Nightlife register where it doesn't ("they know," "help's coming").

**Routing (D14).** §3 mode subtitles already in register ("Quiet, simple, present" / "You have somewhere to be" / "Safer late-night path"). Slice 2 implementation must not regress.

**Push notifications.** Hardest surface. Never sound like a wellness reminder. Never "your wellbeing matters." Never "don't forget your..." Most push copy in HOTMESS is one or two words. "You good?" is a complete push notification.

**Marketplace.** HNH MESS product copy uses lived language, not pharmacy copy. The products are intimate; the copy is too.

**Radio interstitials.** DJ tone, not announcer tone. The radio is in the night with the user.

**Live presence / Go Live.** See D12 §"Surface signposting" for canonical examples. Cold-start copy is the test bed — "You're the first live in [district] tonight" is the model.

## 11. Moderation voice

Worth its own section because moderation is where HOTMESS most easily drifts into HR voice.

The rule: moderation speaks plainly, takes responsibility for the action, and does not euphemise.

- ✅ "This crossed a line." / "We removed it." / "We blocked them." / "We're looking into this."
- ❌ "This content may have violated our community guidelines." / "Action has been taken." / "We have received your report and will review."

Moderation can be firm without being institutional. Care for the reporter without being saccharine. Decision-making without bureaucracy.

The default in moderation is **say less**. A removal with no message often communicates more care than a removal accompanied by three paragraphs of policy.

## 12. Forbidden moves (locks)

Restating the universal forbids as enforcement:

1. **Never use "room"** unless describing a literal physical room.
2. **Never use "wellness"** anywhere. Anywhere.
3. **Never use "self-care"** as a noun phrase in copy.
4. **Never use "journey"** to describe a user's state.
5. **Never apologise pre-emptively** ("Sorry to interrupt...").
6. **Never preface with care-disclaimers** ("If you're struggling...").
7. **Never use "we're here for you."** It is the most institutional phrase in queer wellness software.
8. **Never push a notification that fails the smoking area test.** Notifications are the highest-risk surface.

If a PR introduces any of the above, it does not ship — regardless of how technically correct the rest is.

## 13. The rule (final)

> **Care shouldn't sound sanitised. It should sound like nightlife people taking care of each other in nightlife language.**

Every copy line on a care-adjacent surface answers to this. If you can read a line aloud in the smoking area at 4am and it sounds right, it ships. If it sounds like something HR would write, an NGO would post, or a wellness app would push, it gets rewritten before it ships.

No exceptions for "but it tested well" or "but the grant requires it." Doctrine wins.
